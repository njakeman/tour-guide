interpretation: |
  The textbook answer is grain storage: sealed pits keep seed corn alive over winter. But the deliberate deposits argue the emptied pits were then given back — closing a store with a gift. On this reading Caburn's summit is less a granary than a place where the harvest cycle was negotiated with whatever powers governed it.

# The Crowded Summit

The interior looks empty — a tilted lawn dropping to the southern cliff. It is anything but. You are standing on one of the most intensively dug surfaces of Iron Age Sussex.

## Look Out For

- Shallow dimples in the turf after dry summers — the settled fills of pits below
- The Ouse gap at Lewes, the river's only route through the Downs
- Firle Beacon and, on clear days, the sea at Newhaven

![The Ouse valley from the southern rim](/tours/mount-caburn/ouse-view.png)

> "Every pit was a promise: grain buried against the winter, and something left behind when the promise was kept."

## Full Circle

From here the path drops back north-east to Glynde the way you came — with the whole sequence now legible: the composed approach, the boundary, and the busy, buried summit it protected.
